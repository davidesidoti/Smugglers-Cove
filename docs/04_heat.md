# 04 - The Heat System

The risk pillar. This is the **node where all the other systems meet**: it touches economy (how much dirt you dare do), social (the Navy modulates it), and in first person it's the most adrenaline-charged moment-to-moment. It was designed last on purpose - only with the other pillars in place does it become clear how much it holds them together.

## Guiding principle: Heat is a choice, not a punishment

Every time Heat is high, the player must have *interesting actions to take*, not just "stop having fun and wait." If the only response to Heat is passivity, it's a punitive timer. If high Heat opens a fan of tense choices (bribe? shift the blame? push further? hide?), it's a living mechanic. Keep this compass on everything below.

## The abstract system: how it rises and falls

Heat is a value that grows with dirty actions and decays slowly over time. The finesse is making it **granular** rather than one global bar. Two layers:

- **Global Heat** - your general notoriety with the authorities. Rises with all illicit activity, decays slowly. Determines the base *frequency* of inspections and the Navy's general attitude toward you.
- **Per-activity localized Heat** - the Weaponry where you forge illegal arms has its own heat level; the Ship Repair where you hide pirate ships has another. Makes inspections *targeted* and lets you manage risk per business separately. You can keep the Market spotless as a facade and concentrate risk on the Weaponry, knowing that's where they'll come looking.

Decay is not purely passive. Behaving well (serving many legal ships, supplying the Navy, donations) **accelerates cooling**. This reconnects legal to illicit: legal volume isn't just income, it's *detergent* for your Heat.

## Factors that modulate Heat (beyond your actions)

- **Navy reputation** (see `03_factions_characters.md`): high = Heat grows slowly, inspections rare and announced; low = fast growth, surprise inspections. The direct faction-risk link.
- **Discretion of your actions:** a deal done at night, in a hidden spot, with little goods exposed, generates less Heat than the same deal in broad daylight. Rewards cunning play.
- **Witnesses:** serving a wanted pirate while a Navy ship is docked nearby spikes Heat. Introduces spatial and temporal awareness that becomes pure gameplay in first person.

## The physical experience: the inspection as a set piece

This is what makes the game different from any management sim. The inspection is not a popup with a dice roll - it's a **sequence played in first person**, and it should be the highest-tension moment in the game.

**1. Warning.** You get variable advance notice - long if you have high Navy rep or intel from the Tavern, short or none if you're in bad standing.

**2. Real-time concealment phase.** You must physically secure the illicit goods as the inspector approaches: move contraband crates into the false-bottom, cover illegal cannons with a tarp, douse the clandestine forge, make compromising ledgers disappear, maybe distract the inspector while an NPC handles the rest. The key: *you don't have time for everything*. You must decide what to hide first, what's most incriminating, what to risk leaving. This is midgame Lever 1 (scarce time/attention) turned into an adrenaline set piece.

**3. Confrontation phase.** The inspector walks your businesses, checks, asks questions. Social choices enter: attempt bribery (risky if it's the wrong day or he's feeling honest), bluff with patter, have forged documents that hold up or don't, or just hope the hiding spot holds. If it's the inspector-character you have a relationship with, all of this is charged with your shared history - one you've bribed for months looks the other way; one you've treated badly hunts for the smallest flaw.

## Graduated consequences (never a hard game over)

Getting caught must never be "you lost, restart" - that just angers and switches the game off. Consequences scale with how much and what they find:

- **Fine** for minor infractions - annoying but absorbable.
- **Seizure** of the illegal goods found - lose the investment, not the business.
- **Temporary closure** of a business - cuts an income source for a while.
- **Arrest** at the extreme - but handled with a minigame (escape, or bail paid by burning relationships and money, or a favor called in from a friendly faction).

Losing is always a *setback you recover from*, never a wall. This keeps the player in the game after a failure, which is what makes them come back to risk again.

## The valves: what you do when Heat is high

These are the active choices that make Heat a mechanic, not a punishment:

- **Cool down actively:** a stretch of legal-only activity and donations to the Navy.
- **Bribe** the inspector preemptively to buy soft inspections.
- **Shift the blame** onto a competitor - plant evidence or tip off the Navy to divert attention onto him (ties into the rival-merchant character in `03_factions_characters.md`).
- **Buy intel** from the Tavern to know when an inspection is coming and prepare (ties into the Tavern activity in `02_activities.md`).
- **Accept the risk** and push further, because the big score is worth it.

Each is a decision with a cost and a trade-off; this is where the player expresses their style.

## How Heat ties the whole game together

Look at what this one system touches:
- **Economy** - determines how much dirt you can afford, hence your margin.
- **Factions** - the Navy modulates it, the Pirates push you to raise it.
- **Characters** - the inspector is the face of risk; your relationship with him changes every inspection.
- **Midgame** - it's the source of scarcity (risk) that takes over when money stops creating tension.
- **First person** - it's the most memorable moment-to-moment, the sequence players retell.

Heat is not one system among many - it's the knot where all the others meet.
